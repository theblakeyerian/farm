Fertility program — no synthetic fertilizers

🐟
Compost tea — brewed from on-farm compost
Brewed from the farm's own compost in a 5-gallon bucket with an aquarium pump aerating for 24–36 hours. Apply as a soil drench every 3–4 weeks through the growing season. Delivers active microbial populations and soluble plant-available nutrients directly to the root zone. Compost tea is the regenerative alternative to liquid synthetic inputs — it builds soil biology rather than bypassing it. Cost: essentially free once the composting system is established.
Application: every 3–4 weeks, growing season · brewed from on-farm compost
🍂
Annual fall compost top-dressing
1 inch of finished compost spread across all beds each October. This is the primary mechanism for building organic matter from 1.74% toward the 4–5% target. As organic matter rises, CEC rises proportionally — the soil gradually becomes self-sustaining. By Year 5–7 the composting system largely replaces all purchased inputs. 5–7 year process.
Application: 1 inch across all plots · October each year
🍄
Mycorrhizal inoculant — Mycorrhizal Applications brand
Dust on every transplant root zone at planting. Compensates for the 28% mycorrhizal reading in the soil test (below optimal). Under permanent no-till, mycorrhizal networks build naturally over years — this inoculant accelerates that process at establishment. Never needed after Year 3 in well-established no-till beds.
Application: at planting only · every transplant, Year 0–2
🌿
Comfrey Bocking 14 — chop-and-drop fertility
Plant one Bocking 14 division next to every asparagus row, every berry plant, and throughout the herb beds. Chop and drop the entire plant 4–6 times per season — deep roots mine calcium, potassium, and phosphorus from the subsoil and deposit them at the surface as mulch. Accelerates compost decomposition. Bocking 14 is sterile — will not self-seed aggressively. This is the backbone of the farm's regenerative fertility system — free nutrients from the land itself.
4–6 chops per season · plant next to every perennial · free potassium, calcium, phosphorus
Why regenerative fertility? On a CEC of 2.48, granular synthetic fertilizers partially leach past the root zone before plants can access them. The regenerative approach — deep compost incorporation, comfrey chop-and-drop, and compost tea drenches — builds the soil biology that makes nutrients available naturally. As organic matter rises from 1.74% toward 4–5% over 5–7 years, the soil becomes increasingly self-sustaining. By Year 5, the on-farm compost and comfrey system provides all fertility the garden needs at near-zero cost.

Sheep — Day 1 in 1,500 sq ft paddock
8 sheep graze at high density — not selective. They consume ~25 lbs dry matter, trample ~25 lbs (pressing seeds and organic material into the soil), and leave ~25 lbs untouched for root recovery. Moved to fresh paddock on Day 2. Manure deposits begin immediately — fly larvae hatch in 24–48 hours.
Chickens — Day 2 in same paddock
30 Brahmas move in within 24–48 hours of sheep leaving — before fly larvae mature and pupate. Each bird works ~50 sq ft. They scratch all fresh manure, consume larvae, beetles, grubs, and earthworms, and distribute fertility evenly across the paddock. Also the farm's primary fly control mechanism — no chemicals needed.
Paddock count on 2 acres: 87,120 sq ft ÷ 1,500 sq ft/paddock = 58 paddocks. Each paddock rests 57 days before sheep return — ideal for Virginia pasture recovery and barber pole worm larvae die-off (larvae survive 60–90 days on pasture; 57-day rotation keeps just ahead of re-infestation). At 4 acres the rest period extends to 115 days — excellent parasite management.

How to ferment feed: Add feed to food-grade 5-gallon buckets. Cover with water at a 2:1 water-to-feed ratio by weight. Stir, loosely cover (don't seal — needs to breathe), leave at room temperature 48–72 hours until slightly sour-smelling and bubbling. Feed daily; start the next batch the same day you feed. Fermented water from the bottom of buckets is a beneficial drench that can be diluted and given as drinking water. The system reduces feed cost, improves gut health, and typically increases production 5–10% over dry-fed flocks.

Pruning is not optional in Virginia's humidity. Unpruned blackberries and grapes develop fungal disease that can collapse a planting in one season. Dormant pruning (January–February) is the most important annual task. Schedule it as a fixed commitment in the first week of February every year — before bud swell. How sheep and silvopasture work together

Tree row spacing — designed for sheep
Trees planted 20–25 ft apart in rows with 30–40 ft lane width — wide enough for sheep to graze comfortably underneath. ATV passes between rows for harvest. Sheep graze lane middles, suppressing grass and weeds without mowing. Their manure fertilizes trees — free nutrient delivery to the root zone year-round.
Young tree protection — Years 1–2
Sheep excluded from the drip-line of all new plantings for the first 2 years using electronet fence rings. Spiral plastic bark guards on every tree trunk Years 1–3. After Year 2 trees are tall enough that sheep cannot damage the canopy. Electronet rings are removed and sheep have full lane access permanently thereafter.
Weed suppression — sheep vs. mower
At 14 acres of silvopasture, mowing lane middles = 60–80 hours of labor annually. Sheep at the right stocking rate graze the same middles as part of their daily rotation — zero mowing labor, plus they fertilize as they go. The silvopasture lane becomes part of the rotational grazing system once trees are established.
What sheep must NOT access
Hops bines: Sheep will eat them — keep out entirely.
Grape clusters: Exclude Aug–Oct during ripening.
Young blackberry primocanes: Exclude May–Sep during active cane growth.
Windfall fruit (figs, persimmons): Fine for sheep — cleans up fruit that would harbor disease.
